June 8
St Hedwig, Queen

Optional Memorial

Hedwig, born the year 1374, was the daughter of King Louis, the King of Hungary and Poland. From her father, she inherited the Polish Throne and was crowned in 1384. Marrying the Grand Duke of Lithuania, Władysław Jagieło, she contributed to the conversion of Lithuania to Christianity. She supported the creation of the Krakow Theological institute as well as the renewal of the university. Throughout her whole life, she was marked by generosity to the poor. She died in Krakow on the 17th of July 1399.

The Office of Readings

From the Annals, that is The chronicles of the renowned Polish Kingdom, of Jan Długosz, Canon of the Krakow Cathedral.

Elizabeth, Queen of Hungary, the Widow of Ludwig, finally fulfilled her repeated promise to send Hedwig, her daughter, to Poland, especially when she saw that it was already a dangerous thing for the Poles to prolong their hopes for her arrival. She sent her with a large retinue of lords and knights, among which were Cardinal Dimitri the presbyter of the Four Crowns and Archbishop of Esztergom and Jan, Bishop of the Khanate, with a magnificent royal expedition adorned with gold, silver, vessels and costly robes, jewels, purples and silks.

Upon the news of her arrival, the prelates and Polish Lords, who had already doubted if her arrival was to come at all, hurried to meet her and led her with great joy in a ceremonial procession of the clergy and all states of life to Krakow. Such was the favour of the prelates and Lords of Poland towards her, that with such great and affectionate love, forgetting almost their masculine seriousness, did not feel any disgrace in subjection to such a noble and virtuous woman.

In fact, this motivated their love and favour; that they did not appoint her a spouse, thinking that she herself was enough to rule the kingdom with no need for a husband. Therefore, thus, on the fifteenth day of October, on which the Poles celebrate the feast of St. Hedwig, in the Cathedral of Krakow, Bodzenta, the archbishop of Gniezno, the Bishop John of Krakow, Jan of the See of Włocławek and Dobrogost of Poznan, crowned her. They did so in the presence of the aforementioned Cardinal Archbishop of Esztergom, and a large gathering of Lords and knights of both the Polish and Hungarian kingdoms. They anointed her as the Queen of the Poles, having given her total authority to manage the kingdom. That is until she would given a spouse capable of ruling.

And no wonder: for they knew that she was well brought up and educated, that in her virtues, beauty, charming modesty, splendour of the heart, purity of her renown virginity, and the gentleness and sweetness of her morals, greatly outnumber even that of the noblest families. They saw in her a rare allure, which naturally adorned her. Learned in the sciences, whilst retaining her stateliness she comported herself as a lady, but above all she was temperate and modest. Heaven itself endowed Hedwig with these gifts so much so, that never had the world seen such a woman, but above all the aforementioned modesty dwelled in her, which is the best ornament of a woman. It seemed as though she had drunk in all these virtues with her mother’s milk. Already as a young maiden, she seemed to bear the maturity and wisdom of a matron.

The concluding prayer as at Morning Prayer.

Morning Prayer

Benedictus ant. Today rejoice, O Saint Queen Hedwig, in the name of all the nations of which you are the mother of the faith.

O God, who gave to St Hedwig the gift of love and contemplation of Christ crucified, that she might wisely serve him with love for her brothers, grant even to us that by her intercession we may love you with unwearied love in prayer and in works. Through our Lord Jesus Christ your Son, who lives and reigns with you in the unity of the Holy Spirit, one God, for ever and ever.

Evening Prayer

Magnificat ant. We praise you, O Saint Queen Hedwig, who in your life showed love is the highest of all virtue.
