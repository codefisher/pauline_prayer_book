February 25
St John Cassian, Abbot

Memorial

John Cassian was born around 360 AD, most likely in modern Romania. When he was 20, he came to Palestine and became a monk in one of the monasteries in Bethlehem. Next, he spent 10 years in Egypt among the monks there, where he came to know renowned masters of the eremitical life. Then in the years following he lived in Rome. There he received priestly orders and befriended the future Pope Leo the Great. Around 415, he founded two monasteries, one for monks (St Victor’s) and another for Virgins (The Saviour’s).

Second Reading
A reading from
Conferences of the Fathers by Saint John Cassian, priest and abbot.
(Liber III, Conlatio XVIII, 6)
Of the system of the Anchorites and its beginning.

Out of this number of the perfect, and, if I may use the expression, this most fruitful root of saints, were produced afterwards the flowers and fruits of the anchorites as well. And of this order we have heard that the originators were those whom we mentioned just now; viz., Saint Paul and Antony, men who frequented the recesses of the desert, not as some from faint heartedness, and the evil of impatience, but from a desire for loftier heights of perfection and divine contemplation, although the former of them is said to have found his way to the desert by reason of necessity, while during the time of persecution he was avoiding the plots of his neighbours. So then there sprang from that system of which we have spoken another sort of perfection, whose followers are rightly termed anchorites; i.e., withdrawers, because, being by no means satisfied with that victory whereby they had trodden under foot the hidden snares of the devil, while still living among men, they were eager to fight with the devils in open conflict, and a straightforward battle, and so feared not to penetrate the vast recesses of the desert, imitating, to wit, John the Baptist, who passed all his life in the desert, and Elijah and Elisha and those of whom the Apostle speaks as follows: “They wandered about in sheepskins and goatskins, being in want, distressed, afflicted, of whom the world was not worthy, wandering in deserts, in mountains and in dens and in caves of the earth.” (Heb. 11:37-38) Of whom too the Lord speaks figuratively to Job: “But who has sent out the wild ass free, and who has loosed his bands? To whom I have given the wilderness for a house, and a barren land for his dwelling. He scorns the multitude of the city and hears not the cry of the driver; he looks round about the mountains of his pasture, and seeks for every green thing.” (Job 39:5-7) In the Psalms also: “Let now the redeemed of the Lord say, those whom He has redeemed from the hand of the enemy;” (Ps. 100:2) and after a little: “They wandered in a wilderness in a place without water: they found not the way of a city of habitation. They were hungry and thirsty: their soul fainted in them. And they cried unto the Lord in their trouble and He delivered them out of their distress;” (Ps 106:4-6) whom Jeremiah too describes as follows: “Blessed is the man that has borne the yoke from his youth. He shall sit solitary and hold his peace because he has taken it up upon himself,” and there sing in heart and deed these words of the Psalmist: “I have become like a pelican in the wilderness. I watched and have become like a sparrow alone upon the house-top.” (Ps 101:7-8)

Morning Prayer

Benedictus ant. No one can be my disciple, says the Lord, unless he gives up all his possessions.

Concluding Prayer
O God who in Saint John Cassian
have given us an example of evangelical perfection,
grant we beseech you amidst the trials of our life
we may love the truths of heaven with our whole heart.
Through our Lord Jesus Christ your Son,
who lives and reigns with you in the unity of the Holy Spirit,
one God, for ever and ever.

Evening Prayer

Magnificat ant. Well done, good and faithful servant; come and join in your Master's joy.
