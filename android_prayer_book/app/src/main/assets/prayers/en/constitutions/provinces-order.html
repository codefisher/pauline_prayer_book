Chapter IV: The Provinces of the Order

Article 136

In our Order, in connection to the Historical tradition of the Order, Provinces may be created, in as far the is required by: the Spiritual Good, Development of the Order and other serious reasons.

Province is understood as meaning the union of at least three houses under the direction of a Major Superior that is the Provincial, canonically erected (cf: Can. 621, Can 608-610 and Can. 115 §2).

When erecting a province account should be taken of the capacity of organizational, formational and economic self-sufficiency.

Every Province is governed by its statutes confirmed by the General Chapter or the Father General with the decisive vote of his council.

Article 137

The General Chapter or the Father General with the deciding voice of the Definitorium (Can. 581) erects provinces. This is done by a Decree of Erection in which the geographical territory is described, the number of houses included in its make-up together with the specific mission of the Province. The Father General calls for the first Provincial General Chapter.

A Province is headed by a Provincial chosen at the Chapter. He must have at least ten years of Solemn Profession and must be confirmed by the Father General. Assisting him is a Council composed of four Definitors of whom the first will be the Vicar. Also assisting the provincial is the administrator for the goods of the Province and a secretary.

Article 138

The Provincial and his Council consisting of four Definitors, the secretary and administrator of the Province are chosen according to the norms of the General Directory at the ordinary Provincial Chapter for a period of three years. The first Definitor who must be professed solemnly at least for five years fulfils the function of Vicar of the Province.

The Provincial who first notifies the Father General convenes the Chapter. The Father General either personally or through his delegates has the right of participating in this Chapter. Care must be taken that the time for convening Provincial Chapters should not conflict with times set for General Chapters.

Members of the Provincial Chapter are the following:

  1. The Father General or his delegate, the Father Provincial (or quasi Provincial) with his Council;
  2. Delegates of the whole Province chosen according to our Order’s law should be at least of the same number as those who take part in the Chapter by virtue of the law itself.

The Presider of the provincial Chapter for the entire time of its duration will be the Father General (or his delegate).

Article 139

The authority of the Provincial, who should be a Monk with at least ten years of Solemn Profession, is valid for a full three years and is limited to his Province. He enjoys the powers of a Major Superior. He should be an individual endowed with those qualities expected of a Major superior both by Canon Law and in our Constitutions. His authority extends to things both spiritual and material, namely: Matters pertaining to all the monasteries, persons and those governing in the Province. It belongs to his competence to transfer individual Monks from one monastery to another. The authority to transfer Monks from one Province to another belongs to the Father General. It is the Provincial’s duty to convene and prepare for Provincial Chapters both ordinary and extraordinary. Before taking office the Provincial must take a Profession of Faith (Can. 833, §. 8).

Article 140

It belongs to the duties of the Provincial to make a yearly visitation either personally or through his delegates to all the Houses of the Province. He must also on a yearly basis make a report to the Father General as to the state of his Province.

Although his authority is valid ordinarily for three years, after its expiration, he is eligible to be re-elected for a second three-year period by the same Chapter. A third consecutive three-year term is not allowed.

Article 141

In the event that the above-mentioned conditions for creating a province are not fully established, the General Chapter or the Father General with the consent of his Council may legitimately establish Quasi-Provinces according to Can. 620 and appoint Their Quasi-Provincial and the method of choosing his Council. In other cases, for a just cause the Father General, after obtaining the consent of his council, shall appoint his Delegate whose powers are determined each time by decree.
